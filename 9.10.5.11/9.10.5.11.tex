\section{Problem}
ABC and ADC are two right triangles with common hypotenuse AC. Prove that
$\angle CAD$ = $\angle CBD$.
\section{Solution}
We will use vectors to prove that $\angle CAD = \angle CBD$. \\
Let's assume that A, B, C, and D are the vertices of the two right triangles.\\
We can represent the position vectors of points A, B, C, and D as $\vec{a}, \vec{b}, \vec{c}, and \vec{d},$ respectively. Then we have:
\begin{align}
AB &= \vec{b} - \vec{a}\\
AC &= \vec{c} - \vec{a}\\
CD &= \vec{d} - \vec{c}
\end{align}
Since ABC and ADC are right triangles, we know that:
\begin{align}
AB \cdot AC &= 0 \\
CD \cdot AC &= 0 
\end{align}
substituting 2.0.1 and 2.0.3 in 2.0.5
\begin{align}
CD \cdot AC = 0\\
\brak {\vec{d} - \vec{c}}\cdot AC &= 0\\
\vec{d} \cdot AC - \vec{c} \cdot AC &= 0
\end{align}
Since AC is non-zero, we can divide both sides by $\norm {\vec{AC}}^2$ to get:
\begin{align}
\brak{ \frac{\vec{d} \cdot AC} {\norm {AC}^2 } }- \brak{\frac{\vec{c} \cdot AC} {\norm {AC}^2 } } = 0
\end{align}
But $\frac { \vec{d} \cdot AC} {\norm {AC}^2} = \cos \angle CAD $ and $\frac{\vec{c} \cdot AC} {\norm {AC}^2}= \cos \angle CBD$. Therefore, we have:
\begin{align}
\cos\angle CAD - \cos\angle CBD &= 0\\
\implies  \angle CAD = \angle CBD
\end{align}
